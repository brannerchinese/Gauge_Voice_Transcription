What is this subject?

  • the true common language of China and the “Sinosphere”
  • something that has always been a written langage and never spoken
  • but has had immense influence on educated spoken language
  • known by all educated people in the Far East for over two millennia
  • until less than a hundred years ago, virtually the only language used for writing Chinese
  • known as
    • Wényán 文言 ‘literary language’ or Wényánwén 文言文 ‘writing in the literary language’
    • Gǔ Hànyǔ 古漢語 or Gǔdài Hànyǔ 古代漢語 ‘ancient Chinese’ (a term recently translated from Western languages)
    • Kambun~Hàn-bûn~Hanmun~Hán Văn 漢文 ‘Chinese writing’ in Japanese, Taiwanese, Korean, and Vietnamese, variously.
  • the West calls it "classical" or "literary" Chinese (which are not exactly the same)

The goal of this course and how you will achieve it



  • Goal:
    • To improve your mechanical command of Classical Chinese, through intensive exposure to texts of the pre-Hàn tradition.
  • Means:
    1. Your job is to know all the texts intimately and be sure you understand (not guess) why they mean what they mean.
    2. In class, you will always read from the unmarked text projected on the screen, not from your notes, so prepare well!

Requirements of this course:

  1. Attend all classes. If you aren’t prepared, be here anyway; when you’re called on, just say you aren’t ready. I will not explode. If you really cannot avoid missing a class, I do not need to see a note or hear why you were absent.
  2. Be present mentally, please. Do not use or look at cell phones or email or news or shopping sites at any time while the class is in session. These things have an effect on the whole class.
  3. Be prepared. You should be able to read the texts aloud smoothly and with understanding. That means practicing ahead of time. If you don’t practice, you will probably do a lousy job. Not only you but also I will be embarrassed.
  4. Know every word and every grammar pattern in every text we read — what it means in context, how to pronounce all the words and what part of speech they are. You will be tested for the most part on materials we read in class, so you should know them cold. If you can memorize them, that will certainly help you. Since this is a language class, you should concentrate on craft rather than theory, and on practice rather than originality.
  5. Study with other students. Yes, I know there are many obstacles, but it is very helpful.
  6. I do not expect perfection, but I do expect you to try your hardest to be perfect.

Grading

  1. Grades range from A to F. There is no Pass/Fail option and no auditing in this class (the University does not permit these grades in language classes). No incompletes can be given without a directive to me from the Dean’s Office.

  2. Here is how the University defines the letter grades:“A, excellent; B, good; C, fair; D, poor but passing; F, failure.”

    If you are hoping for an A or a B, the level of your work will naturally have to be far above average.

  3. I do not grade on a curve. Everyone can get an A; everyone can fail. That means you are not in competition with the other students in this class. It is in your interest to study with them and help them to do well, since the better they do, the more they are in a position to help you, too.

  4. Your grade represents my judgment as to your fulfillment of the requirements of this class. It cannot be guaranteed based on the sum of your graded work. In considering your fulfillment of the those requirements, I will count mclassroom performance most heavily, with the final exam second.

What we will read

Depending on the level of the class, we will read famous extracts from three major pre-Hàn thinkers:

  • Mencius (Mèngzǐ 孟子)
  • Zhuāngzǐ 莊子
  • Hánfēizǐ 韓非子

and a historical text, the Zuǒzhuàn 左傳.

But we will begin the class with a review of the material from the first semester, and depending on the level of the class, the content may change.

19/22

Other required materials

  • You cannot do well in this class without at least one proper Classical Chinese dictionary, one made of paper or else scanned from one made of paper.
  • No Mandarin dictionary is adequate for this purpose, because many words have meanings and usage in Wényán that are absent in Mandarin. Avoid Mathews, please, for that reason.
  • You probably won’t find all you need on-line, either, but Zdic.net (which has cribbed the text from the Hànyǔ Dà Zìdiǎn 漢語大字典 and Hànyǔ Dà Cídiǎn 漢語大詞典) is useful.
  • I recommend
    • Gǔ Hànyǔ chángyòngzì zìdiǎn 古漢語常用字字典 (many editions)
    • Wáng Lì Gǔ Hànyǔ Zìdiǎn (Zhonghua Shuju 中華書局, Beijing)
    • Dà Cídiǎn 大辭典 (Sanmin Shuju 三民書局, Taipei; 3 volumes)
    • Cíyuán 辭源 (1970’s edition; 4 volumes)
    • Grand Dictionnaire Ricci de la langue chinoise [Lìshì Hàn-Fǎ Cídiǎn 利氏漢法辭典] (Instituts Ricci, Paris et Taipei; 7 volumes)
